About

My aim is to enable precise forecasting of thermal injury, so that it is predicted and prevented rather than treated after it occurs. More people are exposed to extreme environments today than at any point in recorded history, yet thermal risk is still assessed using population averages and fixed environmental thresholds that do not account for individual physiological variability. To close this gap, I work to establish individualized thermoregulation modeling as a discipline that predicts risk at the level of the person rather than the population.

My modelling work covers the full spectrum of anatomical detail, from high-fidelity 3D virtual humans that resolve heat transfer at the organ level to reduced-order surrogates efficient enough for real-time prediction, and across the human lifespan from the young and healthy to the elderly and even the fetus. By pairing this range with real-time sensing and early warning, I am building toward a future in which every individual, in any thermal environment, can know their own safe limits before those safe limits are crossed.

Research Areas

Individualized Thermoregulation Modeling

This research develops individualized thermoregulation models that move beyond the standard adult to capture heat strain across a broad range of human physiology, including military and defense personnel operating in extreme thermal environments and occupational workers in high-heat industrial settings, to competitive athletes of diverse body morphologies, elderly individuals with diminished thermoregulatory capacity, people experiencing homelessness and those with substance use that elevates heat strain risk, and the near-term fetus during maternal heat exposure. Model complexity spans high-fidelity 3D finite-element representations resolving organ-level heat transfer to reduced-order surrogates validated against human-trial data for real-time field deployment.

Heat Strain Measurement & Early Warning

Translating environmental conditions into physiological risk requires measuring what the human body actually experiences, not just ambient air temperature. This work develops and validates field-deployable platforms that simultaneously resolve mean radiant temperature, turbulent airflow, temperature, and humidity, producing high-fidelity boundary conditions for person-specific, real-time heat strain estimation. Measurements span compact two-cylinder radiometer-anemometers for rapid field deployment to controlled experiments with ANDI, a full anatomical advanced thermal manikin, characterizing human heat exchange under realistic outdoor conditions.

Clothing & PPE Heat Transfer

Workers in military, firefighting, and industrial occupations face a compounded heat burden: their protective clothing creates a microclimate that traps heat and restricts evaporative cooling precisely when the body needs to dissipate it most. This work develops analytical and computational models of heat and mass transfer through clothing, examining dry conduction, moisture-driven phase change, and wet fabric conduction, as well as how human movement drives ventilation and convection through the garment-skin interface. Experimental validation spans firefighter gloves, lower-back exosuits, and occupational workwear, generating the material-level parameters that feed thermoregulation models for realistic occupational heat strain prediction.
