Clothing is the primary interface between the human body and the thermal environment, yet its effects on heat transfer remain complex and difficult to characterize. My research investigates how garments, gloves, and wearable devices modify the microclimate at the skin surface, with implications for occupational safety, athletic performance, and military/first-responder applications.

Using thermal manikins, 3D scanning techniques, and computational modeling, I study the thermal and evaporative resistance of clothing ensembles under realistic conditions, including motion and varying environmental exposures.

## Key Projects

**Glove Microclimate and Area Factor Analysis**

Using 3D scanning methods, I characterized local microclimate properties — including trapped air layer thickness and area factors — for gloves of different types and fits. This work provides improved input parameters for hand thermal models and PPE design standards.

**Thermal Properties of Exosuits**

Wearable assistive exoskeletons are increasingly used in occupational settings, but their thermal impacts on the wearer are poorly understood. I evaluated the thermal resistance and thermoregulatory consequences of a lower-back exosuit using thermal manikin experiments, providing design guidance for thermally safe wearables.

**Clothing Thermal Resistance in Wet Conditions**

Wet clothing dramatically changes its insulative and evaporative properties. This work quantified thermal resistance changes as a function of moisture content, supporting the development of more accurate heat stress models for workers in wet environments.
