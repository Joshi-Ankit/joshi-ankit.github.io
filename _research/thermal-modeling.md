Understanding how the human body responds to extreme thermal environments is central to protecting human health in the face of increasing heat events. My research develops and refines computational thermoregulatory models that simulate heat and mass transfer from the human body under both heat and cold stress conditions.

A key focus is bridging the gap between built-environment measurements and realistic boundary conditions for computational manikin simulations. As part of an NSF-funded project ([Award #2152468](https://www.nsf.gov/awardsearch/showAward?AWD_ID=2152468)), I work on translating field thermal measurements from mobile biometeorological stations into convective and radiative boundary conditions for 3D human body simulations. This work also advances thermoregulatory models to address critical gaps — particularly the influence of air turbulence, non-uniform radiation, and body morphology on heat strain across diverse populations.

## Key Projects

**Dynamic Sensing and Computational Approaches to Assess Individual-level Heat Risk Across Diverse Populations** (NSF Award #2152468)

This project combines advanced field instrumentation with thermal manikin experiments and computational modeling to develop new methods for assessing realistic human heat exposure. My contributions focus on model development, manikin-based boundary condition refinement, and validation against field measurements.

**3D Thermoregulation Model Development**

Development of a whole-body thermoregulatory model incorporating segmental anatomy, blood flow distribution, and sweat response, enabling simulation of individualized thermal responses under various activity levels and environmental conditions.
