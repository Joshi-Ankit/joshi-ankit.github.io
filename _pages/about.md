My aim is to enable precise forecasting of thermal injury, so that it is predicted and prevented rather than treated after it occurs. More people are exposed to extreme environments today than at any point in recorded history, yet thermal risk is still assessed using population averages and fixed environmental thresholds that do not account for individual physiological variability. To close this gap, I develop individualized thermoregulation models and the early-warning systems they enable. These models cover the full range of human thermal modeling, from high-fidelity 3D virtual humans that resolve heat transfer at the organ level to reduced-order models efficient enough for real-time prediction. Each is tailored to individual physiology across diverse populations, from the young and healthy to the elderly and even the fetus.

### 2023 – 2025
**Research Scientist** · [School for Engineering of Matter, Transport & Energy](https://semte.engineering.asu.edu/), Arizona State University (ASU)

My research at Arizona State University (ASU) from 2023 to 2025 advanced three interconnected areas, each essential to the overarching goal of understanding and predicting how the human body responds to thermal extremes.

**(a) Individualized thermoregulation modeling** — extending bioheat models beyond the standard adult to capture physiologically distinct groups, including the elderly, the fetus, and young healthy populations, and using these models to evaluate the effectiveness of heat mitigation strategies such as fan cooling, water dousing, and cold-water immersion.

**(b) Real-time environmental and physiological sensing** — developing mobile instrumentation that characterizes the thermal microclimate people actually experience and couples those measurements directly to thermoregulation models, enabling person-specific heat strain estimates in the field.

**(c) Experimental characterization of human heat and mass transfer** — using thermal manikins to measure convective and radiative exchange under realistic outdoor conditions, including the effects of turbulence and body morphology, generating the validated coefficients that make computational models more accurate and broadly applicable.

### Pre-2023
**Postdoctoral Researcher & PhD** · Iowa State University & [Empa — Swiss Federal Laboratories for Materials Science and Technology](https://www.empa.ch/) (Dübendorf, Switzerland)

Prior to ASU, I completed my PhD and postdoctoral work at Iowa State University and Empa — Swiss Federal Laboratories for Materials Science and Technology (Dübendorf, Switzerland) — where my focus was on how clothing and personal protective equipment (PPE) mediate heat and mass transfer between the human body and the environment. This work spanned analytical and computational modeling of garment thermal properties, wet conduction and moisture transport within clothing layers, and experimental characterization using thermal manikins — building the physical foundation for the thermoregulatory modeling work that followed.
